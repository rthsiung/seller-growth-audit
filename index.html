Key insights

Five patterns that emerge from comparing how these products approach seller performance understanding and action guidance.

01
The "why gap" is the biggest shared design failure
Most analytics surfaces show what happened — sales down 12%, sessions up 8% — but not why. The apps that earn the highest ratings invest in decomposition: waterfalls, funnels with per-step conversion rates, source breakdowns. They treat the number as the beginning of a question, not the answer. This is the clearest design opportunity for Etsy: connect performance readings to explanatory signals sellers can actually act on.
Stripe — MRR waterfall HubSpot — funnel conversion rates Oura — readiness contributor breakdown Wave — cash flow category comparison
02
Context transforms recommendations from noise to signal
Generic "things to try" lists rarely drive action. The highest-rated action flows surface the right recommendation at the moment of maximum receptivity — immediately after a campaign sends, right after setup completes, when a metric dips. Timing and context are the real differentiators, not the content of the recommendation itself. A great suggestion shown at the wrong moment is still ignored.
HubSpot — metric-triggered tooltip Klaviyo — AI insight below flagged metric Whoop — coaching text below each metric Wave — Advisor bar adjacent to transactions
03
Benchmarks are the missing link in most performance views
Without a reference point, a seller can't know whether their numbers are good. A 4% open rate, 120 daily sessions, or 43 engagements is meaningless in isolation. Mailchimp's three-way benchmark (this campaign / your average / industry) and Pinterest's audience-vs-population view are rare exceptions. Most sellers don't know if their numbers are good or bad — the product should tell them.
Mailchimp — industry benchmarking Whoop — personal 30-day average as benchmark Klaviyo — Healthy / Needs attention thresholds Wave — year-over-year cash flow comparison
04
Progress framing consistently outperforms feature menus
Shopify's staged checklist and Duolingo's streak system outperform every app that surfaces growth as a menu of features to explore. Wrapping actions inside a visible progress arc — with a named goal at the end — reduces cognitive load and creates forward momentum. The specific action matters less than the frame around it. Sellers need to feel like they're moving toward something, not choosing from an infinite options list.
Shopify — staged setup guide Duolingo — streak + XP system Klaviyo — Action Center checklist with Mark done
05
Analytics and actions are siloed when they need to be one surface
In every app audited, performance data and growth recommendations live in separate navigation sections. The mental gap between "I see my sales are down" and "here's what I can do about it" is left entirely to the seller to bridge — and most don't. This is the single largest shared failure across the products audited, and the most significant design opportunity for the Etsy seller platform.
Partially bridged: HubSpot, Klaviyo, Wave Well bridged: Whoop, Oura Fully siloed: Stripe, TikTok, Pinterest, Instagram, Depop, Substack, eBay, Squarespace, Wix, Gumroad
Design patterns

What works well

Patterns observed across multiple products that reliably help sellers understand performance or take action.

Effective pattern
Decomposition chart
Breaking a top-line number into its contributing factors — MRR into new/expansion/churn, traffic into source channels, conversion into funnel stages — surfaces the "why" that aggregate metrics obscure.
Why it works: Sellers can't act on "revenue was down." They can act on "churn increased 3% while new customers held steady." Decomposition turns a status report into a diagnosis.
Stripe MRR waterfall Oura readiness contributors Wave cash flow by category HubSpot deal funnel Squarespace purchase funnel
Effective pattern
Benchmark anchoring
Showing a metric alongside a reference point — industry average, your own prior period, comparable users — gives sellers the calibration they need to judge whether a number is good.
Why it works: Absolute numbers are uninterpretable without a baseline. Benchmark anchoring turns a number into a signal by answering the question sellers always have: "Is this good?"
Mailchimp campaign benchmark Whoop personal 30-day average Klaviyo Healthy / Needs attention
Effective pattern
Contextual action trigger
Surfacing a recommendation at the moment it's most relevant — post-send, post-setup, when a metric dips — dramatically increases the likelihood it will be acted on.
Why it works: Relevance is a function of timing as much as content. A recommendation shown at the right moment feels helpful; the same recommendation shown at the wrong moment feels like noise.
Klaviyo AI insight below metric Whoop coaching below sleep deficit Wave Advisor bar beside transactions
Effective pattern
Staged progress tracker
Breaking a large goal into labeled stages with visible progress gives sellers a clear "you are here," reduces overwhelm, and creates completion momentum across stages.
Why it works: Completing one stage creates the motivation to start the next. Sellers feel like they're advancing toward a goal — even before reaching it — which is more sustaining than an undifferentiated to-do list.
Shopify setup guide Duolingo streak calendar
Effective pattern
Impact quantification
Attaching a specific, credible outcome estimate to a suggested action — "2.2x more views," "23% higher open rate" — makes prioritizing between options tractable.
Why it works: Sellers face many possible improvements. Impact estimates let them compare options and choose the highest-leverage move rather than the most obvious or most recently suggested one.
LinkedIn profile suggestions Mailchimp content score Depop "2x more views" discount nudge
Effective pattern
Loss aversion framing
Warning users about what they stand to lose — a streak, a momentum indicator, a hard-won status — is more motivating than showing what they'll gain from taking action.
Why it works: Loss aversion is roughly 2× more motivating than equivalent gain framing. The highest-engagement consumer apps exploit this systematically. The risk is that overuse feels manipulative.
Duolingo streak warning Duolingo Streak Society lock
Effective pattern
AI cold-start removal
Replacing the blank-canvas moment with a conversational prompt, pre-populated examples, or AI-generated starting point eliminates the hardest part of getting started.
Why it works: Decision paralysis is highest when nothing exists yet. Any starting point — even an imperfect one — is easier to react to and refine than nothing. AI makes this starting point feel personalized rather than generic.

Design patterns

What to avoid

Patterns that appear frequently across products but consistently fail to help sellers understand performance or take meaningful action.

Anti-pattern
Metrics without context
Displaying numbers without comparators, benchmarks, or trend context leaves sellers unable to assess whether their performance is good, bad, or expected.
Why it fails: Numbers without context create false confidence as often as appropriate concern. A seller with a 24% open rate doesn't know if they should be pleased (industry average: 21%) or alarmed (their personal average: 31%).
Pinterest overview Depop earned/potential tiles Substack post stats Patreon patron count eBay traffic metrics Gumroad MRR/customers header
Anti-pattern
Analytics–action separation
Putting performance data and growth recommendations in separate navigation sections forces sellers to mentally bridge insight to action — a gap most don't cross.
Why it fails: The insight ("traffic is down from Search") and the action ("optimize titles and tags") should be one step. Separated by navigation, they feel like unrelated jobs and the connection is never made.
Stripe Depop Substack Patreon eBay Squarespace Wix Gumroad
Anti-pattern
Feature-level recommendations
Recommending specific features ("try pop-ups," "create a coupon") rather than outcomes assumes sellers already know their goal and just need to find the right button.
Why it fails: Most sellers need to know why to try something before they'll act. Feature recommendations skip the motivation step entirely, leaving sellers to supply the strategy on their own.
Square growth hub LinkedIn Premium cards
Anti-pattern
Activity-proxy progress
Measuring and rewarding task completion rather than actual outcomes creates a false sense of progress and can optimize for busywork over meaningful improvement.
Why it fails: Completing 8 setup tasks doesn't mean a seller is closer to their first sale. Outcome-based progress ("you've had 50 profile views this week") is harder to implement but more honest and more motivating.
Duolingo XP Shopify checklist
Anti-pattern
Premium-gating growth tools
Positioning meaningful growth features exclusively behind a paid tier signals that improvement requires payment rather than effort, and erodes trust in the free experience.
Why it fails: Sellers who feel nickel-and-dimed disengage from the platform entirely. Growth tools should feel like a partnership. When they feel like a funnel, sellers stop reading recommendations.
LinkedIn Premium hub
Anti-pattern
Untimely or generic recommendations
Surfacing recommendations at arbitrary moments or without personalization — "here are some things you could try" — trains sellers to ignore them entirely.
Why it fails: Recommendation fatigue sets in quickly. Once a seller learns that suggestions aren't tailored to their situation, they stop reading them — and that trained inattention is very hard to reverse.
